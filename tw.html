Introduction

Technical Writers

About This Guide

This writing style guide helps everyone at KUBRA communicate with clarity, consistency, and confidence. As a Technical Writer, you produce the documentation, procedures, and reference materials that customers and colleagues rely on to get things done.

Good writing builds trust. When our content is consistent, readers can focus on understanding the message and taking action.

This guide covers goals and principles, tone and voice, language, grammar and mechanics, sentence structure, and abbreviations and acronyms — all tailored to the Technical Writing team.

Writing Goals & Principles

Core Principles

Empower

Give readers the confidence and tools to act. Make the next step clear and achievable.

Respect

Use people-first language. Value the reader’s time and avoid unnecessary complexity.

Educate

Focus on what users need to know to move forward. Prioritize usefulness over completeness.

Guide

Be friendly, helpful, and solution-oriented. Reduce uncertainty and friction wherever possible.

For Technical Writers

Your Focus
  • Thoroughness — Cover prerequisites, document steps, explain validation, include troubleshooting.
  • Clarity of scope — State what the content covers and what it does not at the beginning of each document.
  • Reusability — Structure procedures, warnings, notes, and examples so they can be reused across documents.
  • Consistency — Use a standard structure for similar content so readers know what to expect.

Examples

Empower the Audience

× Before

“This tool supports various functions.”

✓ After

“You can use this tool to create reports, automate workflows, and manage customer data.”

Respect the Reader

× Before

“Obviously, you need to configure your settings first.”

✓ After

“Before you begin, configure your notification settings.”

Educate through Clarity

× Before

“This tool’s UI is hard to navigate for novices.”

✓ After

“New users can find guidance through step-by-step tutorials and an intuitive interface designed to simplify navigation.”

Guide with Specificity

× Before

“Fill in the form completely.”

✓ After

“Enter your full name, email address, and phone number in the fields provided.”

Tone & Voice

Voice

Voice defines KUBRA’s personality. It remains consistent across channels while allowing flexibility based on audience and context.

Core Attributes

Confident but Humble

We know our space and can help, without sounding arrogant.

Informal but Precise

We sound human and approachable, but never sloppy or unclear.

Helpful, Never Overbearing

We guide readers toward outcomes without pushing or overwhelming them.

Your Voice

Technical Writer Voice

Use an expert guide voice. Writing should sound knowledgeable and practical, using active voice and clear ownership of actions.

Voice Examples

× Technical — Vague

“Do the password thing to get back in.”

✓ Technical — Clear

“To reset your password, enter your email and follow the link provided in your inbox.”

Tone

Tone is how voice adapts to the situation. While KUBRA’s voice stays consistent, tone should shift based on context, audience, and emotional stakes.

Core Stance

  • Tone is generally informal, but always clear. Clarity takes priority over entertainment.
  • Tone should adapt to user context — troubleshooting, exploration, or decision-making.

Your Tone

Technical Writer Tone

Use a neutral, professional tone. Procedures should be objective and predictable, while troubleshooting content should show empathy and focus on recovery.

Tone Examples

× Error Message — Blunt

“Connection failed.”

✓ Error Message — Empathetic

“Your connection was lost. Please check your network and try again.”

× Difficult Task — Dismissive

“Just follow the steps.”

✓ Difficult Task — Reassuring

“This process has a few steps, but we’ll walk you through each one.”

Inclusive Language

  • Avoid words or phrases that might alienate diverse groups.
  • Use “allowlist” instead of “whitelist.”
  • Use “they” for gender neutrality.
  • Avoid age markers (e.g., “elderly”).

Inclusive Language Examples

× Don’t

“Add the domain to the whitelist.”

✓ Do

“Add the domain to the allowlist.”

× Don’t

“When a user logs in, he will see his dashboard.”

✓ Do

“When a user logs in, they will see their dashboard.”

Language

Language choices determine how easy content is to understand, scan, and trust. At KUBRA, language should feel clear, consistent, and inclusive across all content types.

Core Language Approach

  • Use plain language whenever possible. Choose familiar words and direct verbs over complex or abstract phrasing.
  • Be consistent. Use one term per concept and apply it everywhere.
  • Write inclusively. Avoid language that assumes identity, role, ability, or experience.
  • Write for a global audience. Avoid slang and metaphors. Use clear date and time formats.
  • Use American English by default. Canadian spelling is used only when content is explicitly targeted to a Canadian audience.

For Technical Writers

Your Language Approach

Prioritize precision and consistency. Match UI labels and system messages exactly, define technical terms when first introduced, and use acronyms carefully.

Language Examples

Plain Language

× Before

“Utilize the aforementioned functionality to facilitate the process.”

✓ After

“Use this feature to complete the process.”

× Before

“Commence the initialization procedure.”

✓ After

“Start the setup.”

Consistency

× Inconsistent

Using “dashboard,” “home screen,” and “main page” interchangeably for the same view.

✓ Consistent

Always refer to it as the “dashboard” — one term, used everywhere.

Global Audience

× Before

“Let’s knock this out of the park by EOD.”

✓ After

“Let’s complete this by 5:00 PM ET today.”

American vs. Canadian English

× Canadian (default content)

“Colour,” “centre,” “analyse”

✓ American (default)

“Color,” “center,” “analyze”

Grammar & Mechanics

Grammar and mechanics create consistency across KUBRA content and reduce reader effort.

Core Conventions

  • Use the Oxford comma.
  • Use parentheses for asides.
  • Follow consistent rules for numbers, dates, and times.
  • Avoid decorative punctuation.
  • Format UI elements consistently.

For Technical Writers

Your Mechanics Focus

Consistently format UI labels, code, and commands. Always include a clear recovery path in troubleshooting content.

Grammar & Mechanics Examples

Oxford Comma

× Without

“KUBRA offers billing, payments and notifications.”

✓ With Oxford Comma

“KUBRA offers billing, payments, and notifications.”

Numbers & Dates

× Inconsistent

“The report covers three months starting 3/10/26.”

✓ Consistent

“The report covers 3 months starting March 10, 2026.”

UI Element Formatting

× Unclear

“Click submit to save your changes.”

✓ Clear

“Click Submit to save your changes.”

Troubleshooting Recovery Path

× Dead End

“The upload failed.”

✓ Recovery Path

“The upload failed. Check that your file is under 10 MB and in .csv format, then try again.”

Parentheses for Asides

× Before

“The feature — which was released last quarter — is now available to all users — including trial accounts.”

✓ After

“The feature (released last quarter) is now available to all users, including trial accounts.”

Sentence Structure

Sentence structure affects how quickly content can be read and understood. Structure should support scanning, clarity, and action.

Core Approach

  • Prefer short, direct sentences.
  • Express one idea per sentence and one topic per paragraph.
  • Place the most important information first.
  • Use lists to reduce complexity and make content easier to scan.

For Technical Writers

Your Structure Approach

Keep procedures atomic, with one action per step and verb-first instructions. Headings should describe outcomes, not topics.

Sentence Structure Examples

Short & Direct

× Before

“In order to be able to access the reporting dashboard, users will first need to make sure that they have completed the setup process and verified their account information.”

✓ After

“Complete setup and verify your account to access the reporting dashboard.”

One Idea per Sentence

× Before

“Click Save, which will store your preferences and also send a confirmation email to your address on file and update the audit log.”

✓ After

“Click Save to store your preferences. A confirmation email will be sent to your address on file. The audit log updates automatically.”

Verb-First Instructions

× Before

“The configuration file should be opened by the administrator.”

✓ After

“Open the configuration file.”

Outcome-Based Headings

× Topic-Based

“Password Information”

✓ Outcome-Based

“Reset Your Password”

Using Lists

× Dense Paragraph

“To get started, you need to create an account, verify your email, set up two-factor authentication, and configure your notification preferences.”

✓ Scannable List

To get started:
1. Create an account.
2. Verify your email.
3. Set up two-factor authentication.
4. Configure your notification preferences.

Abbreviations & Acronyms

Abbreviations and acronyms can improve efficiency when used carefully, but they can also create confusion when overused or introduced without context.

General Guidance

  • Spell out on first use, followed by the shortened form in parentheses.
  • Do not introduce acronyms that appear only once or twice on a page.
  • Avoid internal or team-specific shorthand in customer-facing content.
  • When multiple acronyms appear in the same section, take extra care to avoid cognitive overload.

For Technical Writers

Your Abbreviation Approach

Acronyms are often unavoidable, but they should always be introduced intentionally. Define them clearly, especially when they represent systems, roles, or configurations that affect task outcomes. Consider whether the acronym is industry-standard or product-specific, and define accordingly.

Abbreviation & Acronym Examples

First-Use Introduction

× Before

“Configure the API to connect to the CRM.”

✓ After

“Configure the Application Programming Interface (API) to connect to the Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system.”

Avoid One-Time Acronyms

× Unnecessary

“The Key Performance Indicator (KPI) showed improvement.” (KPI never appears again)

✓ Clearer

“The key performance indicator showed improvement.”

Internal Shorthand

× Internal Jargon

“Submit a PRT to the CSE team for triage.”

✓ Customer-Friendly

“Submit a product request ticket to the Customer Success Engineering team for review.”

Industry-Standard vs. Product-Specific

× Assumed Knowledge

“Enable SSO via the IdP settings in the admin console.”

✓ Defined Clearly

“Enable Single Sign-On (SSO) through the Identity Provider (IdP) settings in the admin console.”
