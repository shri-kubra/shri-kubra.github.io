Introduction

UX Writers

About This Guide

This writing style guide helps everyone at KUBRA communicate with clarity, consistency, and confidence. As a UX Writer, you shape the words users see inside our products — labels, tooltips, error messages, and every piece of microcopy that guides them forward.

Good writing builds trust. When our content is consistent, users can focus on their tasks instead of deciphering our interface.

This guide covers goals and principles, tone and voice, language, grammar and mechanics, sentence structure, and abbreviations and acronyms — all tailored to the UX Writing team.

Writing Goals & Principles

Core Principles

Empower

Give readers the confidence and tools to act. Make the next step clear and achievable.

Respect

Use people-first language. Value the reader’s time and avoid unnecessary complexity.

Educate

Focus on what users need to know to move forward. Prioritize usefulness over completeness.

Guide

Be friendly, helpful, and solution-oriented. Reduce uncertainty and friction wherever possible.

For UX Writers

Your Focus
  • Microcopy focus — Every label, tooltip, and message should help users understand what to do next.
  • Context awareness — Reflect the current UI state and user progress in system messages.
  • Brevity — Keep copy short and focused, especially in constrained spaces.
  • Predictive assistance — Anticipate common questions and address them before they interrupt the flow.

Examples

Empower the Audience

× Before

“This tool supports various functions.”

✓ After

“You can use this tool to create reports, automate workflows, and manage customer data.”

Respect the Reader

× Before

“Obviously, you need to configure your settings first.”

✓ After

“Before you begin, configure your notification settings.”

Encourage Engagement

× Weak CTA

“Learn more.”

✓ Strong CTA

“Discover how this feature can improve your workflow.”

Tone & Voice

Voice

Voice defines KUBRA’s personality. It remains consistent across channels while allowing flexibility based on audience and context.

Core Attributes

Confident but Humble

We know our space and can help, without sounding arrogant.

Informal but Precise

We sound human and approachable, but never sloppy or unclear.

Helpful, Never Overbearing

We guide readers toward outcomes without pushing or overwhelming them.

Your Voice

UX Writer Voice

Act as a friendly companion in the interface. Writing should feel conversational, supportive, and focused on helping users move forward.

Voice Examples

× UX — Wordy

“You might want to press here to proceed.”

✓ UX — Concise

“Press to continue.”

Tone

Tone is how voice adapts to the situation. While KUBRA’s voice stays consistent, tone should shift based on context, audience, and emotional stakes.

Core Stance

  • Tone is generally informal, but always clear. Clarity takes priority over entertainment.
  • Tone should adapt to user context — troubleshooting, exploration, or decision-making.

Your Tone

UX Writer Tone

Use a conversational tone in normal flows, a calm and reassuring tone in error states, and a brief, positive tone in success messages.

Tone Examples

× Error Message — Blunt

“Connection failed.”

✓ Error Message — Empathetic

“Your connection was lost. Please check your network and try again.”

× Success — Flat

“Done.”

✓ Success — Positive

“All set! Your changes have been saved.”

Inclusive Language

  • Avoid words or phrases that might alienate diverse groups.
  • Use “allowlist” instead of “whitelist.”
  • Use “they” for gender neutrality.
  • Avoid age markers (e.g., “elderly”).

Inclusive Language Examples

× Don’t

“Hey guys, check out the new feature!”

✓ Do

“Hello everyone — check out the new feature!”

× Don’t

“This feature is so easy, even your grandma could use it.”

✓ Do

“This feature is designed to be intuitive for users of all experience levels.”

Language

Language choices determine how easy content is to understand, scan, and trust.

Core Language Approach

  • Use plain language whenever possible.
  • Be consistent. Use one term per concept and apply it everywhere.
  • Write inclusively. Avoid language that assumes identity, role, ability, or experience.
  • Write for a global audience. Avoid slang and metaphors.
  • Use American English by default.

For UX Writers

Your Language Approach

Mirror how users think and speak. Use familiar language, clear action verbs, and concise phrasing that supports quick decision-making in the interface.

Language Examples

Plain Language

× Before

“Utilize the aforementioned functionality to facilitate the process.”

✓ After

“Use this feature to complete the process.”

Consistency

× Inconsistent

Using “dashboard,” “home screen,” and “main page” interchangeably.

✓ Consistent

Always refer to it as the “dashboard” — one term, used everywhere.

Inclusive Language

× Before

“Even a beginner can figure this out.”

✓ After

“This feature is designed to be straightforward for all experience levels.”

Grammar & Mechanics

Grammar and mechanics create consistency across KUBRA content and reduce reader effort.

Core Conventions

  • Use the Oxford comma.
  • Use parentheses for asides.
  • Follow consistent rules for numbers, dates, and times.
  • Avoid decorative punctuation.
  • Format UI elements consistently.

For UX Writers

Your Mechanics Focus

Use sentence case for UI text, keep punctuation minimal, and maintain consistent message patterns across similar interactions.

Grammar & Mechanics Examples

Sentence Case for UI

× Title Case

“View Your Account Settings”

✓ Sentence Case

“View your account settings”

Decorative Punctuation

× Decorative

“Welcome to KUBRA!!!”

✓ Clean

“Welcome to KUBRA.”

Oxford Comma

× Without

“KUBRA offers billing, payments and notifications.”

✓ With Oxford Comma

“KUBRA offers billing, payments, and notifications.”

Sentence Structure

Sentence structure affects how quickly content can be read and understood.

Core Approach

  • Prefer short, direct sentences.
  • Express one idea per sentence and one topic per paragraph.
  • Place the most important information first.
  • Use lists to reduce complexity and make content easier to scan.

For UX Writers

Your Structure Approach

Keep labels and messages short. Sentence fragments are acceptable when context makes meaning clear.

Sentence Structure Examples

Short & Direct

× Before

“In order to be able to access the reporting dashboard, users will first need to make sure that they have completed the setup process.”

✓ After

“Complete setup to access the reporting dashboard.”

Important Information First

× Before

“After reviewing the documentation and consulting with your team lead, submit the request form.”

✓ After

“Submit the request form after reviewing the documentation.”

Abbreviations & Acronyms

Abbreviations and acronyms can improve efficiency when used carefully, but they can also create confusion when overused.

General Guidance

  • Spell out on first use, followed by the shortened form in parentheses.
  • Do not introduce acronyms that appear only once or twice on a page.
  • Avoid internal or team-specific shorthand in customer-facing content.
  • When multiple acronyms appear in the same section, take extra care to avoid cognitive overload.

For UX Writers

Your Abbreviation Approach

Avoid acronyms in UI text whenever possible. Short interface copy should prioritize immediate understanding. Acronyms may be appropriate in tables, advanced settings, or constrained spaces only when the audience is familiar with them.

Abbreviation & Acronym Examples

UI Text — Avoid Acronyms

× In UI

Button label: “Generate SOW”

✓ In UI

Button label: “Generate statement of work”

Acronym Overload

× Overloaded

“The CSR used the CRM to update the SLA per the SOW before notifying the PM via the IVR.”

✓ Readable

“The customer service representative updated the service-level agreement in the CRM, following the terms in the statement of work.”
